Cheese Rain

Using cheese to predict the weather

As a long time enjoyer of both hard cheeses and rainy days, the claim that cheeses wrapped in plastic will “sweat” profusely before a large storm is of great interest to me. I set out to investigate whether this adage has any scientific basis.

Interestingly enough, the initial search of “cheese sweats” references gustatory sweats - a consequence of the protein caesin, which causes the human body to release dopamine upon consumption of cheese and other milk products. Some reports speculate this is an evolutionary incentive for infants to consume their mother’s milk. Cheese also contains amino-acids that, when metabolised, produces phenethylamine, a substance which is commonly compared to amphetamines (yknow, like the drug). While this is a fascinating tangent that no doubt explains the cheese-shaped hole in my wallet, it does not yet explain how I can use cheese to predict the weather.
here is an image in case you don't know what cheese is
At a certain temperature, air is capable of holding a certain amout of water vapour before it is considered “saturated”. Meteorologists use the “dewpoint” temperature - the temperature a body of air must be cooled to become saturated - to determine if precipitation will occur. Of course they are usually wrong, but nevertheless, we have our answer. Since air is more humid and saturated with water vapour before significant rainfall, then more vapour will precipitate out when we wrap our cold cheeses.

R. Affine, (19-17)
